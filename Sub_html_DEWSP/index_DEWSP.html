Deeply Equal-Weighted Subset Portfolios



Backgound and motivation
Financial economists have long sought strategies to maximize the expected return for a given level of risk. Research has established modern portfolio theory (MPT) and its extensions, which provide theoretical models for optimal strategies and key concepts such as diversification and risk-return tradeoff. The methods are considered a standard approach in the field of investment research. Although investors should utilize these tools in their investment decision-making, they usually do not. This is because their decisions are influenced by psychology rather than financial theory. Unfortunately, psychological biases hinder one’s ability to make successful investment decisions. We develop a new model to be used as a roadmap or guidance on decision-making to enable investors to overcome the biases and increase their wealth.

Owing to their limited practicability, investors are reluctant to actively employ theoretical portfolio models. The optimization methods are often of a “black box” nature since it is hard to understand the relationship between input and output variables. As a result, investors are sometimes not fully aware of the output being applied, leading them to distrust or discount them. More importantly, increasingly sophisticated technologies do not necessarily lead to better outcomes than naïve and simple methods, which have frequently been better than using complex ones [1]. This is one of the main reasons the use of complex methods in investment practice is still quite limited.

To mitigate these problems, we increase the transparency of outcomes for practitioners and validate the method. This allows us to check the prerequisites of investment, such as whether outcomes are too unusual to accept and are required to be modified according to the needs of investors. We design a portfolio model to mimic the significant investing behaviors of individual investors using machine learning. Such an approach helps investors accept the model and its results more easily since the basis is familiar and allows easily evaluation of the outcomes in terms of investors’ own perspectives. Thus, it helps investors to mitigate problems stemming from cognitive errors, psychological biases, and emotions by comparing to quantitative results produced by machine learning.
Our model
Our model is based on the recent paper by Lee (2020), who proposes Deeply Equal-Weighted Subset Portfolios (DEWSPs) [2]. DEWSPs are subsets of top-N assets selected based on the rank of the return forecasts from deep neural networks, where the members are equally weighed. It is based on two primary patterns in individual investing behavior. First, most individual investors own only a small number of stocks and only sell stocks that they own. In the process, they are essentially making a prediction about the company’s risk and return relative to others. Second, they prefer to weight the stocks equally, rather than relying on complex optimization techniques. In the absence of better information, equal weighting arises naturally in the allocation-decision process. We adapted the two behaviors as a backbone to construct our model. Figure 1 graphically represents the schematic diagram of building DEWSPs and their risk and return properties on a risk-return plane. From the observations (to be discussed later), the following important characteristics are highlighted:
  • The concave-like frontier results from diversifying assets,
  • the equal-weight portfolio of the whole asset is the baseline of the DEWSPs, and
  • the historically equal-weighted subset portfolios (HEWSPs) are the benchmarks of DEWSPs.


Performance
Figure 2 presents the results of the DEWSPs on a risk-return plane. DEWSPs are overly superior to the benchmark of both HEWSP-TV and HEWSP-V in terms of higher returns given levels of risk. As the number of assets decreases, portfolio returns increase from the baseline of the EWP of whole assets, which shows a risk-return tradeoff known in the stock market.
We evaluate the performance of DEWSPs by measuring the average monthly Sharpe ratio improvement rate (SRIR) relative to the benchmark of HEWSPs over all Ns. The positive values of the SRIR (13%–27%) reveal the benefit of the DEWSPs, indicating the following features: (1) a transparent mechanism based on a risk-return tradeoff, (2) ability to tailor portfolio risk and return by controlling portfolio size N with respect to a baseline of EWP and a benchmark of HEWSP, and (3) ability to build portfolios using small numbers of assets (with expandability to large assets)
